Overview

This workshop guides you through developing effective questions for your upcoming user testing sessions. By the end, you will have a complete set of testing questions ready to use for Playtest 1.

Since you're testing early-stage, in-progress work, your focus is on validating core ideas and fundamental functionality before investing more development time. You're not testing polish or completeness.

What to Test at This Stage

Note: These are general project categories. Your work may incorporate aspects of multiple types, and it is fine to mix testing methods to fit your specific project.

Test these things now:

Games: Core mechanics, control schemes, challenge comprehension
Installations: Spatial understanding, interaction discovery, cause-and-effect
Static/Motion Media: Visual hierarchy, message comprehension, pacing
UX/Apps: Task comprehension, navigation clarity, mental model alignment

Save these for later:

Visual polish, complete content, all features, performance optimization, edge cases, final materials

Step 1: Define Your Prototype 5 min

A. Select your primary medium:

  • Game / Interactive Digital
  • Installation / Interactive Experience
  • Static Media (Poster, Graphic)
  • Motion Media (Animation, Video)
  • UX/App Project

B. List what exists in your prototype right now (2-3 things)

C. List what's missing that testers should ignore (2-3 things)

D. Write your framing statement:

"This is an early [prototype/mockup] of [describe project]. It doesn't have [missing elements] yet. I want to learn: [your focus]. Please focus on [what to pay attention to], not [what to ignore]."
Step 2: List Your Assumptions 10 min

Write 5-7 assumptions about how users will interact with your work. These become the basis for your questions.

  • "Users will understand that..."
  • "People will notice..."
  • "Visitors will try to..."

Rank each by risk:

  • Critical: If wrong, project fundamentally fails
  • Important: Significant problems occur
  • Minor: Still mostly works

1.0 Credit: You need two testing sessions. Identify what makes them different: different aspects of the project, different user groups, different prototype states, or different features.

Step 3: Generate 8-10 Draft Questions 15 min

Transform your Critical and Important assumptions into open-ended questions. Generate more than you need - aim for 8-10 questions. You will narrow down later.

Question Writing Rules:

Avoid Use Instead
Yes/No: "Is the navigation clear?" "How would you get to [section]?"
Leading: "Don't you think this is intuitive?" "What would you try first?"
Preference: "Do you like this?" "What does this communicate to you?"
About polish: "Is this pretty?" "What do you notice first?"

Use the Starter Questions by Medium section below for inspiration.

Step 4: Peer Review - Narrow to 4 Questions 10 min

Pair up with a classmate (ideally working in a different medium). Share your 8-10 questions and help each other select the best 4.

For each question, evaluate:

  • Does it test something critical to your project's success?
  • Is it open-ended (not yes/no)?
  • Is it non-leading (doesn't suggest the answer)?
  • Does it test something that exists in your prototype (not missing features)?
  • Would a user understand it without explanation?

Cut questions that:

  • Overlap with other questions (testing the same thing twice)
  • Test minor assumptions rather than critical ones
  • Are about features that don't exist yet
  • Would require explaining your project to answer
Step 5: Refine Your Final Questions 5 min

Take your 4 selected questions and refine the wording. Make each question as clear and specific as possible.

Refinement checklist:

  • Remove jargon or project-specific terms users won't know
  • Make task-based questions specific: "Find the settings" not "Navigate around"
  • Add context if needed: "From where you're standing..." or "After watching that..."

Organize by testing phase:

Pre-Test (1 question): Context before they see the prototype
"Have you experienced interactive installations before?"
During-Test (2 questions): While they interact - use Think Aloud prompts
"What are you trying to do right now?" / "What do you expect will happen?"
Post-Test (1 question): Reflection after the interaction
"What was the hardest part?" / "Would you use this again?"
Step 6: Review Testing Resources 5 min

Browse the Developing User Testing Resources page and find at least one resource that you think will be useful for your testing.

This could be a guide on question writing, a method you want to try, or background reading on testing for your medium.

If none of the listed resources fit your needs, you can find your own via the OCAD Library. Include the resource(s) you used in your discussion post.

Starter Questions by Medium

Use these as inspiration when generating your draft questions:

Installation / Interactive Experience

  1. "What is this?" (concept comprehension)
  2. "How would you interact with it?" (interaction model)
  3. "Where would you stand/position yourself?" (spatial relationship)
  4. "What do you think causes it to respond?" (cause-effect understanding)
  5. "How does being in this space make you feel?" (emotional impact)
  6. "Could another person interact at the same time?" (social dynamics)
  7. "How close feels comfortable?" (spatial comfort)

Static Media (Poster, Graphic, Information Design)

  1. "What do you notice first?" (hierarchy)
  2. "What is this communicating?" (message clarity)
  3. "From where you're standing, what can you read?" (readability)
  4. "What's the mood or feeling of this?" (aesthetic appropriateness)
  5. "Who is this for?" (audience appropriateness)
  6. "What action should you take after seeing this?" (call to action)
  7. "Walk me through how your eye moves across this" (scanning pattern)

Motion Media (Animation, Video, Motion Graphics)

  1. "What just happened?" (sequence comprehension)
  2. "Was that too fast, too slow, or just right?" (pacing)
  3. "Where were you looking?" (attention tracking)
  4. "Could you follow the whole sequence?" (continuity)
  5. "Would you watch this again?" (engagement)
  6. "What happened first, second, third?" (sequence recall)
  7. "Which part was most memorable?" (key moments)

UX/App Project

  1. "How would you [complete specific task]?" (task completion)
  2. "Where would you look for [feature]?" (navigation)
  3. "What do you expect this button/link to do?" (mental model)
  4. "Can you get back to where you started?" (orientation)
  5. "What's confusing about this?" (pain points)
  6. "What can you do on this screen?" (screen comprehension)
  7. "How would you describe how this app is organized?" (information architecture)

Game / Interactive Digital

  1. "Describe how this works in your own words" (mechanic comprehension)
  2. "How does it feel when you [core action]?" (feedback quality)
  3. "What makes this easy or hard to control?" (control clarity)
  4. "What tells you whether you succeeded or failed?" (feedback comprehension)
  5. "What would you try next?" (player agency)
  6. "If you were teaching someone, what would you tell them?" (mental model)
The Think Aloud Protocol

During your test, ask participants to verbalize their thoughts in real-time. This is the single most valuable usability testing method.

Tell testers:

"Please say out loud everything you're thinking as you [interact/use/view] this. Tell me what you're looking at, what you're thinking, what you're trying to do, and how you're feeling."

Your only prompts during the test:

  • "Keep thinking out loud"
  • "What are you thinking right now?"
  • "What did you expect to happen?"
  • "Tell me more about that"

Do NOT:

  • Answer their questions
  • Explain how things work
  • Confirm if they're "doing it right"
  • Guide them toward solutions
When Testers Comment on Incompleteness

When testing outside of class, testers may comment on unfinished elements. These phrases can help bring the conversation back to what you are actually testing:

"This is just cardboard" / rough materials
Acknowledge it, then ask about form and scale instead
"The images are placeholders"
Ask what grabs their attention or whether the hierarchy works
"The animation is choppy" / not polished
Clarify you are testing timing or sequence, not smoothness
"This button doesn't work"
Note that it is not built yet, then refocus on what is working
"The design is basic"
Confirm you are testing functionality first, then return to the task
Example Post (0.5 Credit)

Medium: Interactive Installation

Framing Statement:
"This is an early mockup of a shadow-reactive projection piece. It doesn't have the final projection mapping, sound design, or motion tracking yet. I want to learn if people understand they can interact with it by moving their bodies. Please focus on whether you know what to do, not on the visual quality."

Testing Questions:

Pre-test: "Have you experienced interactive art installations before?"

During-test: "What do you think causes the visuals to change?"

During-test: "Where would you position yourself to interact with this?"

Post-test: "How does this space make you feel?"

Testing Plan Outline:

  • Where: Graduate Gallery, set up with projection on back wall
  • Who: 4-5 classmates unfamiliar with the project + 2-3 people from outside the program
  • How long: 10-15 minutes per participant
  • Prototype state: Full-scale projection mockup with Wizard of Oz interaction (manually triggering responses)

Resources Used:

  • Interactive Architecture Lab: Meaningful Group Experience - helped me think about multi-user dynamics
Example Post (1.0 Credit)

Medium: UX/App Project

Framing Statement:
"This is a wireframe prototype of a recipe sharing app. It doesn't have visual design, real content, or all features yet. I want to learn if the navigation makes sense. Please focus on whether you can find things, not how it looks."

Session 1 - Navigation Testing:

Pre-test: "What recipe apps do you currently use?"

During-test: "How would you find recipes using ingredients you already have?"

During-test: "Where would you look to save a recipe for later?"

Post-test: "How would you describe how this app is organized?"

Session 2 - Recipe Following Flow:

Pre-test: "How do you typically follow recipes while cooking?"

During-test: "Walk me through how you'd cook this recipe using the app"

During-test: "What information do you need that you can't find?"

Post-test: "What would make this easier to use while actually cooking?"

What differentiates the sessions: Session 1 tests whether users can navigate and find features (browsing mode). Session 2 tests whether the recipe-following experience works when someone is actively cooking (task mode with divided attention).

Testing Plan Outline:

Session 1:

  • Where: Quiet space with phone/tablet for prototype testing
  • Who: 5 participants who regularly cook at home, recruited via social media
  • How long: 20 minutes per participant
  • Prototype state: Clickable Figma wireframes with main navigation and browse features

Session 2:

  • Where: Actual kitchen environment (home kitchens)
  • Who: Same 5 participants from Session 1
  • How long: 30-40 minutes (time to actually cook a simple recipe)
  • Prototype state: Updated wireframes with step-by-step recipe view and timer features

Resources Used:

  • Maze Guide: Usability Testing Questions - used to check for bias in my question wording
  • Nielsen Norman Group: Usability Testing 101 - helped structure my task-based questions
