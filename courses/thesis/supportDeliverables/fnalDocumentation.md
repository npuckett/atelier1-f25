# Overview
A standalone website is one of the most effective ways to share and preserve your work. It provides a clear way for your work to be seen and shared at scale, and it allows people to engage with it without *you* having to be there.

# Tone and Style
The key to creating a website for your project is to keep it professional. This is not a school assignment, it is your work. Show it and talk about it as professional work that represents you beyond the classroom. If you aren't sure where to start, find projects / people you like and look at how they show and talk about the work.

## Language, jargon, and marketing speak
A tricky balance with web text is being confident without sounding like a cheesy commercial. AI-generated text tends to be the worst offender -- overly confident, vague, and full of filler. If you use it, rewrite aggressively. It is always better to just describe the project as simply and clearly as possible.

You should have a clear 1 sentence description of your project.

You should have a clear 100 - 200 word description of the project that covers its most important aspects in slightly more depth. As an overview, you don't really get much more text than that. People won't read it. You will likely have more to say and that can be broken out into different sections.

## Documentation
With any media/interactive project it is critical to have documentation in the form of excellent images or videos. 

- Physical Works
Capture the work in a neutral setting with proper lighting. If it is interactive, show people interacting with it. Art direct them to get the shots you need.

- Screen based 
Even if it is a screen-based work, you need to document it.
    - Screen grabs of key moments
    - Screen recordings of play / interaction
    - Image / Videos of people playing with it. This is often a good trick to make it feel more 'real'

## Structure
Projects often exist in several forms and platforms and your website is a clear way to organize all of those things in one place. While each of yours will be slightly different, there are some simple guidelines to follow. The easiest way to go about it is to break down the work into key components, giving people multiple entry points into the work -- medium, genre, technical approach, visual style, etc.

### Homepage
This is the most important part of the site. It is what people see first, so it needs to explain the project clearly. This should involve the best image / video of your work and your intro text. Essentially, a person should be able to *get* the work without looking anywhere else. This can't show every detail, so you need to decide what is the most relevant.

You should also include links to every other place the project exists online -- social media, itch, Youtube, Vimeo, Steam, Github, etc. This is essential for making your site the central hub for the work.



### Supporting Pages
These pages are your chance to show more behind the scenes details on the work. Some examples of this could be
- Technical Development
What are key techniques you have developed for the project. Be sure they are explained visually

- Art
How are the visuals created? It is important to show process, but not overwhelm, just show the best stuff.

- Concept / Narrative
This is your chance to talk a bit more about the 'why' of your work. Where did the concept come from, how did you develop it, why is it interesting.

- About
Who is the person/people that created the work. Give a short bio and links to all of your contact / social media.

## Style
Don't overdesign the website. Keep to a clean style that shows the work clearly and gets out of the way. Simpler/Cleaner is always better. You will want to have this site for a while, so go for something a bit more timeless.

- What about site builders and templates?
Site builders are a solid option, especially if you aren't a web person. They provide clean visual style with minimal effort.

### Some places to look
- Squarespace [https://www.squarespace.com/] A big brand, but the templates are clean and well-designed
- Cargo Collective [https://cargo.site/] More focussed on artists / designers. Lots of good templates to start from
- Building it from scratch. If you are confident in your web skills, you can build it from scratch. Just create a good system that helps you manage it going forward.

# FAQ
- My project exists as a website, do I need to make a website for my website : Yes. Just because the work itself is on the web, you still need something that explains it.

- I already have a portfolio website. Can I just add this as another page: Yes, that is totally fine, just be sure it gets enough space.

- Do I *HAVE* to buy a domain name: No, but it is a good idea in the longer run. They aren't that expensive.
